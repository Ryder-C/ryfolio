I was working on my game [Ryguessr](/projects/ryguessr) when I came to the problem of choosing random colors for players. Naively, I tried:

- **Picking a random hex color**, led to players sometimes getting near-identical colors and no one can tell the pins apart on the map.
- **Rotating through a fixed amount of "nice" colors**, works until a 9th player joins.

I wanted something that (a) produces a stream of colors that are _actually_ perceptually distinct, (b) dynamically makes color choices even after players change their color or leave the game, and (c) has very low computational cost. Almost everything is in one file: [`server/src/colors.rs`](https://github.com/AdinAck/ryguessr/blob/main/server/src/colors.rs) so feel free to check it out.

## Working in a perceptual color space

Humans don't see distances in color the same way that monitors emit it. Two pairs of sRGB colors can be the same Euclidean distance apart and one pair may look very perceptually different compared to the other. So choosing random colors in this type of space is only going to make things more difficult.

The fix is to move into a **perceptually uniform color space**, where Euclidean distance accurately tracks perceived difference.

<Callout
  title="Perceptually Uniform Color Space"
  variant="definition"
  defaultOpen={false}
>
  A color space where the Euclidean distance between two points is proportional
  to how different those colors look to human eyes.
</Callout>

I settled on [**OKLAB**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oklab_color_space), a
successor to [**CIELAB**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CIELAB_color_space) with
some fixes. The conversion is also very cheap, just two $3 \times 3$ matrices
and a cube.

### OKLAB &rarr; LMS &rarr; linear sRGB

OKLAB is built on top of a [LMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LMS_color_space), a color space that models the response of the three types of cones in the human eye: L (long-wavelength, red), M (medium-wavelength, green), and S (short-wavelength, blue).

Here is the pipeline:

{/* prettier-ignore */}
<Callout variant="notation">
    - $L_\text{ok}$ is perceptual lightness (0 = black, 1 = white).
    - $a$ is the green–red axis (negative green, positive red).
    - $b$ is the blue–yellow axis (negative blue, positive yellow).
</Callout>

1. First apply the matrix for OKLAB &rarr; LMS

$$
\begin{pmatrix} L' \ M' \ S' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 &
0.3963377774 & 0.2158037573 \\ 1 & -0.1055613458 & -0.0638541728 \\ 1 & -
0.0894841775 & -1.2914855480 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} L_\text{ok} \\ a \\ b
\end{pmatrix}
$$

<Callout variant="intuition">
  The first column of the transition matrix is all 1's because L (lightness)
  contributes equally to all three cone types.
</Callout>

2. Then cube

$$
L = (L')^3, \quad M = (M')^3, \quad S = (S')^3
$$

3. Then apply the matrix for LMS &rarr; linear sRGB

$$
\begin{pmatrix} R \\ G \\ B \end{pmatrix} =
\begin{pmatrix}
4.0767416621 & -3.3077115913 & 0.2309699292 \\
-1.2684380046 & 2.6097574011 & -0.3413193965 \\
-0.0041960863 & -0.7034186147 & 1.7076147010
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} L \\ M \\ S \end{pmatrix}
$$

Here is the code describing this process:

```rust title="server/src/colors.rs"
fn oklab_to_linear_srgb(lab: [f64; 3]) -> [f64; 3] {
    let [l, a, b] = lab;

    // Step 1
    let l_ = l + 0.3963377774 * a + 0.2158037573 * b;
    let m_ = l - 0.1055613458 * a - 0.0638541728 * b;
    let s_ = l - 0.0894841775 * a - 1.2914855480 * b;

    // Step 2
    let l = l_.powi(3);
    let m = m_.powi(3);
    let s = s_.powi(3);

    // Step 3
    [
        4.0767416621 * l - 3.3077115913 * m + 0.2309699292 * s,
        -1.2684380046 * l + 2.6097574011 * m - 0.3413193965 * s,
        -0.0041960863 * l - 0.7034186147 * m + 1.7076147010 * s,
    ]
}
```

### Linear sRGB &rarr; sRGB (gamma encoding)

Monitor sRGB is gamma-encoded. The forward transfer function is

$$
c = \begin{cases} 12.92 \, c_{\text{lin}} & c_{\text{lin}} \le 0.0031308 \\[4pt] 1.055 \, c_{\text{lin}}^{1/2.4} - 0.055 & c_{\text{lin}} > 0.0031308 \end{cases}
$$

Which we can see in our code:

```rust title="server/src/colors.rs"
fn linear_to_srgb(c: f64) -> f64 {
    let c = c.clamp(0.0, 1.0);
    if c <= 0.0031308 {
        12.92 * c
    } else {
        1.055 * c.powf(1.0 / 2.4) - 0.055
    }
}
```

We clamp each channel into $[0, 1]$ because there exist points in OKLAB that translate to points outside of the sRGB space. This is easy but it's not a true perceptual nearest-point projection, so far out colors can shift hue slightly. This shouldn't really be visible in practice though.

The proper way to do this is explained by Björn Ottosson (the creator of OKLAB) in [one of his blog posts](https://bottosson.github.io/posts/gamutclipping/).

## Sampling OKLAB: the $R_2$ quasirandom sequence

Now I have a nice perceptual space that I can convert to sRGB.

So how do I generate an unbounded sequence of points $\{p_1,p_2,p_3,...\}$ such that each new point $p_{k+1}$ maximizes its minimum distance to all previous points $\{p_1,...,p_k\}$?

What we really want is a [low-discrepancy sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-discrepancy_sequence) (Also called a quasirandom sequence).

<Callout variant="definition" title="Discrepancy">
  Discrepancy is a measure of how uniformly a sequence fills a space. A
  low-discrepancy sequence fills the space more uniformly than random sampling.
</Callout>

### Why the $R_2$ sequence?

We need a two dimensional low-discrepency sequence. But wait, OKLAB is a three dimensional space. Shouldn't we be sampling in three dimensions?

{/* prettier-ignore */}
<Callout variant="recall">
    OKLAB is three dimensional:
    - $L$ (lightness)
    - $a$ (green-red)
    - $b$ (blue-yellow)
</Callout>

We want all player colors to be equally readable on the map. If we let lightness vary randomly then some players would get washed-out colors and other dark muddy colors.

<Callout variant="solution" defaultOpen={false}>
  We can fix this by just sampling from a two dimensional slice of OKLAB where
  $L$ is fixed to some value we choose.
</Callout>

So we only need to fill the $a$-$b$ plane. A natural first idea is the $R_1$ sequence. Given the golden ratio $\varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$, the sequence is defined as

$$
    x_n = \frac{n}{\varphi} + x_0 \mod 1
$$

where $x_0$ is a seed offset. This sequence works beautifully in one dimension:

<R1NumberLine client:load />

So why not just run two $R_1$ sequences, one for $a$ and one for $b$? Well, we end up with points that are nicely spaced when projected to the $a$ or $b$ axis, but when looking at them in two dimensions we see obvious clumping along diagonal lines:

<QuasirandomComparison client:load />

The $R_2$ sequence fixes this by generalizing the golden ratio to two dimensions using the [plastic ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_ratio) $g \approx 1.3247$.

<Callout variant="remark">
  The plastic ratio is the real root of $x^3 = x + 1$. It is also the smallest
  [Pisot number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisot-Vijayaraghavan_number).
</Callout>

$R_2$ uses two offsets derived from the plastic rato.

$$
    \alpha_1 = \frac{1}{g}, \quad \alpha_2 = \frac{1}{g^2}
$$

The sequence is then

$$
\begin{split}
    \mathbf{p}_n &= \left(n \cdot \alpha_1 + x_0,\; n \cdot \alpha_2 + y_0\right) \mod 1 \\
    &= \left(\frac{n}{g} + x_0,\; \frac{n}{g^2} + y_0\right) \mod 1
\end{split}
$$
